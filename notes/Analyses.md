# Excerpts, Analyses, Insights


General insights:
- Dialectic vs. rhetoric:
    - Truth-seeking via clarification of concepts vs. instrumental use of inherent ambiguity in language as a means to certain ends
    - Socratic investigation vs. sophistry
    - Logos vs. Mythos
- Distinction between dialectic and elenchus:
    - Elenchus: method of refutation of false propositions. Leads to a state of perplexity (aporia). Common in early dialogues.
    - Dialectic: constructive method aimed at establishing positive propositions. Leads to a doctrine. Common in middle-late dialogues.
- Human Law (Nomos) and Natural Law (Physis)
    - Sophists considered these two to be fundamentally distinct, leading to moral relativism (morality as a free human construction) and a power-driven instrumental view of social laws ("might makes right")
    - Plato's thesis is that both are products of a common underlying order, that the seemingly arbitrary Human Law has its origins in the absolute Natural Law 
- Kinds of love:
    - Eros
    - Philia
    - Agape
- Aporia: state of psychological perplexity as a result of logical contradiction in one's worldview
- Multiplicity of the human soul and the potential for its unification:
    - Human organism as a set of processes and drives, each with their own goals
    - Goodness is that under which a person restores internal order and becomes a coherent individual
    - The goal of personal morality and justice is ultimately a political one -- of uniting these forces within oneself under a single ruler through diplomacy, negotiations, rewards, and punishments.
- Unity of the True, the Good, and the Beautiful

## Republic

### Book 1

- Polemarchus:
    - Proposition: Justice is doing good to friends and harm to enemies
    - Refutation: Justice is to treat something rightly. Like treating a horse to improve it's horse-ness. And doing something to a person that increases his capacity/virtue as a person. But, to harm a person is to diminish him as a person. So that cannot be justice. QED

- Thrasymachus:
    - Proposition: Justice is the advantage of the strong
    - Socrates' refutation: justice turns out to be the advantage of the subject not the art
    - Proposition: Justice is well-bred simplemindedness, injustice is goodness of judgement
    - Socrates' refutation: A just man/city/group is not at odds with itself nor with other just entities and so the just entity is capable of action. Whereas, a purely unjust entity is completely impotent.
